About the painting

A compact, closed composition marking a turning point in Kandinsky's practice — the moment the circle moved to the foreground as his primary subject. Twenty-six overlapping circles of varying sizes and colours sit within a bold outer ring, many intersected by fine black lines. Two diagonal beams of teal and gold cross through the centre, shifting the colour of every circle they touch.

Painted while teaching at the Bauhaus in Weimar, the work reflects his move away from spontaneous gesture toward deliberate geometric composition — and his belief that colour and form could encode emotion, reflecting the harmony of the cosmos.

"The circle is the synthesis of the greatest oppositions. It combines the concentric and the excentric in a single form, and in balance."

— Wassily Kandinsky
